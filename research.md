# Slide Deck Research: How Professionals Build Decks

Research on methods used by top consulting firms (McKinsey, BCG, Bain), executives, and senior technical staff.

---

## 1. Core Philosophy

**Answer first. Evidence second.**

Professionals present top-down: lead with the conclusion, then support it. This is the inverse of how most people think (bottom-up analysis → conclusion), but it's what executive audiences need. They want the recommendation, not the thought process.

> "You think from the bottom up, but you present from the top down." — Barbara Minto

---

## 2. The Pyramid Principle (Minto, McKinsey)

The foundational framework behind all MBB (McKinsey, BCG, Bain) communication. Developed by Barbara Minto at McKinsey in the 1960s. Now standard at consulting firms, investment banks, and Fortune 500 companies.

```
           [ANSWER / RECOMMENDATION]
          /           |            \
    [Key Point 1] [Key Point 2] [Key Point 3]
       /    \       /    \       /    \
   [Data] [Data] [Data] [Data] [Data] [Data]
```

**Rules:**
- Single main message at the top
- 3 supporting arguments in the middle (MECE — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)
- Evidence/data at the bottom
- Every supporting point must answer: "Why should I believe the title above?"

## 3. SCR / SCQA Narrative Frameworks

### SCR (Situation → Complication → Resolution)

McKinsey's narrative hook. Creates tension and urgency. Used to structure the opening of a deck or the arc of the whole story.

| Component | Role | % of Deck |
|-----------|------|-----------|
| **Situation** | Shared context — what is currently true | 10-15% |
| **Complication** | Problem, change, or tension disrupting the situation | 15-25% |
| **Resolution** | Recommended solution + next steps | 60-70% |

**Example (Netflix pitch):**
- Situation: Netflix grew from $1B to $38B revenue through content investment
- Complication: US market saturated, content costs doubled, pricing ceiling reached
- Resolution: International markets + local originals (30% of watch time) offer next growth vector

### SCQA (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer)

Minto's extended version. Adds an explicit Question to bridge Complication → Answer.

- **Situation**: Establish shared context
- **Complication**: What changed or is at stake
- **Question**: What must be decided or done?
- **Answer**: Your recommendation (this becomes the deck's spine)

## 4. Ghost Deck Method (Pre-Work Process)

Top consultants never open PowerPoint first. They build a **ghost deck** — a skeleton of titles and headlines only, before any content or design.

**Steps:**
1. Write recommendation as one sentence
2. List 3-5 supporting arguments (become section headers / chapters)
3. Write action titles for every supporting slide
4. Read titles alone — does the argument hold? Does the story cohere?
5. Only then open PowerPoint

**Why:** Tests argument structure without design distraction. Minimizes wasted work if the direction changes. Aligns stakeholders before committing to design.

**Time allocation (MBB standard):** 40% structure → 30% analysis → 30% design

## 5. Slide-Level Rules (MBB Standards)

### Action Titles
- Complete sentence (not a topic label)
- ≤15 words, max 2 lines
- States the key takeaway of that slide
- Quantitative when possible
- Active voice

| Bad (topic title) | Good (action title) |
|---|---|
| "Market Overview" | "German market growing 12% annually, 3× faster than US" |
| "Cost Analysis" | "Current ops spend 40% above industry median, fixable in 2 quarters" |

**Test:** A partner can flip through a 50-slide deck reading only the titles and understand the complete argument.

### One Message Per Slide
- Each slide = one insight
- If title contains "and" → split into two slides
- Multiple messages = multiple slides

### Pyramid Per Slide
```
[Action title — the conclusion]
    [Supporting argument 1] [Supporting argument 2] [Supporting argument 3]
              [Evidence]            [Evidence]            [Evidence]
```

### Visual Standards
- **Fonts:** Max 2 (one for titles, one for body). McKinsey: Arial body + Georgia titles
- **Colors:** Max 3-4. Use accent color to highlight data only. High contrast required
- **Alignment:** All elements on a grid. Titles never shift position across slides
- **White space:** Leave breathing room. Never cram content
- **No decoration:** No clip art, stock photos, 3D effects, gratuitous animations

### Data Credibility
- Every data point has a source (e.g., "Source: Company filings 2022–2024; McKinsey analysis")
- Unsourced numbers = not credible

### 60-Second Rule
Each slide must be fully presentable in <60 seconds. If not, it's too complex.

### Standalone Rule
Slides must communicate completely without a presenter. They are documents, not speaking aids.

## 6. Firm-Specific Styles

| Element | McKinsey | BCG | Bain |
|---------|----------|-----|------|
| Title style | Action title + optional subtitle | Action title only, no subtitle | Clean, minimal |
| Visual density | Text-heavy, structured | Chart-heavy, visual | Balanced |
| Accent color | Blue | Green | Red |

Core principles identical across all three. Style differences are minor.

## 7. BCG's 5-Step Storylining + 5-Dimension Slide System

### Storylining (Architecture)
1. Apply Pyramid Principle to frame the central question
2. Calibrate for audience's decision-making style
3. Draft executive summary to test coherence
4. Build slide-flow architecture (action titles + rough sketches)
5. Review and iterate for coherence and impact

### Slide Writing (Execution)
1. **Purpose** — what function does this slide serve? (data, context, process)
2. **Title Architecture** — complete thought driving home the key message
3. **Slide Body** — pick format that matches purpose (chart/table/graphic/text)
4. **Visual Emphasis** — use color/placement/white space to guide attention
5. **Quality Control** — verify accuracy, consistency, readability, alignment

**Squint test:** Key message still clear when viewed from distance or squinting.

## 8. Duarte Method (Storytelling Approach)

Nancy Duarte's framework is used for persuasive/visionary pitches, not just analytical reports.

### The Sparkline
A rhythm of contrast between **"what is"** and **"what could be"**:
```
What IS → What COULD BE → What IS → What COULD BE → ... → New Bliss
```
Creates emotional investment. Mirrors the Hero's Journey. Triggers action.

### 3-Act Structure
- **Act 1:** Current reality — establish stakes
- **Act 2:** Conflict/tension — contrast, challenge, the messy middle
- **Act 3:** Resolution — the call to action, the new future

### Audience as Hero
The audience is the hero; the presenter is the guide. The antagonist is the audience's challenge. Frame everything through their perspective and decisions.

## 9. Executive Presentation Rules

### What Executives Need
- Recommendation on slide 1 or 2 (never buried at end)
- Business outcome connection — every recommendation maps to a consequence
- Technical jargon reduced — if a CFO can't follow it, it fails
- Technical detail in appendix — not in the main deck

### Common Failure Modes
1. Academic structure: building to conclusion at the end
2. Topic titles instead of action titles
3. Multiple insights per slide
4. Missing sources
5. No business impact stated
6. Filling every pixel — no white space

### Tailoring to Audience
- Ask the decision-maker: "How do you prefer to receive information?"
- Senior technical staff: more data density acceptable, fewer business-translation layers
- C-suite/board: recommend first, data second, technical detail in appendix

---

## 10. Practical Workflow Summary

```
1. Define the single recommendation (one sentence)
   ↓
2. Build SCR narrative arc (Situation / Complication / Resolution)
   ↓
3. Write ghost deck: action titles only, no content
   ↓
4. Validate story by reading titles alone
   ↓
5. Add data/evidence to each slide
   ↓
6. Apply visual standards (2 fonts, 3-4 colors, grid alignment)
   ↓
7. Source every data point
   ↓
8. 60-second test per slide
   ↓
9. Squint test for visual clarity
   ↓
10. Executive summary slide (restate SCR in compressed form)
```
